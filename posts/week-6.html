"ThinkleCad" – The AutoCAD for 2nd Graders

My experience with TinkerCad was neutral – not as bad as I imagine App Inventor to be (although I didn't use it for our app project), but not as good as any other professional platform. Like you said from the beginning, TinkerCad was made for kids, and it was made very clear, very quickly to all of us.

My hatred for the platform aside, this definitely wasn't the most useless, or annoying project I have ever completed while at FSU – try taking any classes in the College of Business and you'll see that it can get significantly worse. Building something that theoretically could be real was quite interesting, although I questioned if there aren't more efficient alternatives than manually linking blocks and union grouping voids.

With that in mind, my first thought was how could I leverage AI to make 3D objects by coding instead of manually designing blocks, and that's when I came across OpenSCAD. The platform looks like it is straight out of the .com bubble era, and after asking you whether we could use any other alternatives for TinkerCad and getting told "no," that was the end of that endeavor.

I then pivoted into how I could use AI in TinkerCad itself, going back and forth with ClaudeCode on how to get this done. Using OpenClaw, I devised a set of standard questions it would ask me as a guideline for what I would like to create, bridging my imagination to the real world as accurately as possible. OpenClaw would then generate the 3D object and send the file to me via email, for me to then open it on the platform by importing the file (button on the top-right-hand corner).

Most of the objects came out extremely well – significantly better than anything I could do myself, but worse than a professional engineer using AutoCAD. The point of using AI to make these objects wasn't to avoid doing the work myself, as ultimately it took more time to figure out, and I knew that I would end up doing it myself to avoid risking losing any points.

The point of all of this was learning how to leverage new technologies to automate what was historically done by hand, by students sitting in a classroom. From my view, any opportunity I can to embrace a new technology and try leveraging it to do the same tasks as before is an immense learning experience, and the only way of remaining competitive in a rapidly changing environment.

This project was a fun one, not because I made chopsticks using basic shapes, but because I learned how to think in a 3D plane, started questioning how to leverage standard objects to make unconventional shapes, and most importantly, how to leverage AI to do all that 100x faster.
